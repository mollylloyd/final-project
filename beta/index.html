JAPAN’S TRIPLE DISASTER: The earthquake, tsunami and nuclear meltdown that shook the world and became the most costly natural disaster in history

Three years have passed since the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake — the most powerful earthquake ever recorded to hit Japan — and its accompanying tumultuous events, a 40.5 meter tsunami and the resulting Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster. With over 15,000 lives lost, nearly as many injuries, some 300,000 displaced residents, the destruction of major coastal areas, a nuclear disaster only rivaled by Chernobyl, and an estimated cost of $235 billion, Japan’s Triple Disaster remains at the forefront of global conscience with consequences that will last generations.

We are creating a website in remembrance of the disaster to assist the Japanese and the world in understanding the events surrounding it and how best to move forward. We love this project and will continue developing it (at FukushimaJapan.org) even after CS-171 ends.

Massive quake hits Japan

On Friday, March 11, 2011 a magnitude 9.0 earthquake approximately 70 km off shore violently shook the Western coast of Japan.

Massive quake hits Japan

It was the most powerful earthquake to ever hit Japan, and the fifth most powerful ever recorded in the world.

Tsunami hits Iwate, Miyagi and Fukushima

The massive quake triggered a powerful tsunami that inundated much of the Western coast, travelling up to 6 miles inland in some parts of the country, leaving death and destruction in its wake.

Tsunami hits Iwate, Miyagi and Fukushima

Almost one million buildings and homes were damaged or destroyed, leaving hundreds of thousands homeless and in shelters.

Tsunami triggers most costly nuclear meltdown ever

The 14 meter high tsunami wave that struck Fukushima overwhelmed the 10 meter sea wall protecting the Fukushima Nuclear Power Plant. The water flooded the back-up diesel generators that were powering the reactors’ cooling mechanisms, leading to explosions and melt-down in four of the plant’s six reactors.

The release of radiation following the meltdown led the government to order the evacuation of all people in a 20km radius from the plant—forcing almost 400,000 from their homes and into shelters.

Natural Disasters Across the Globe

The economic and human toll of natural disasters is felt across the globe as their frequency increases.

While Japan's Triple Disaster became the most costly natural disaster in history, loss of life was moderate compared to other events such as the 2010 Haiti earthquake and the tsunami in Sri Lanka in 2004.

Interestingly, loss of life and loss of capital tend to be inversely related, an indication of the development gap between wealthy and poor countries. While disaster preparedness is important for all countries, those with fewer resources could benefit from the contingency plans and emergency response measures that their more wealthy neighbors already have in place.
